# DAOs produce politicians instead of builders because token voting rewards attention-seeking and coalition formation over execution quality

Stani Kulechov — founder of Aave, the largest DeFi lending protocol with $40B+ TVL and $1T+ in originated loans — identifies a specific governance pathology after nearly a decade of running what is arguably DeFi's most mature DAO:

> "DAOs become politicized very quickly and it's easy for voting to become about attention. Participants take sides, lean toward the loudest voices, and form political alliances to get their own proposals passed later. Just as large companies end up with managers instead of founders, DAOs end up with politicians."

This is not a theoretical critique — it comes from the person who has operated the highest-stakes DAO governance system in DeFi for longer than almost anyone. The mechanism is straightforward: when governance power comes from token voting, the scarce resource is attention and coalition support, not execution quality. The skills that win governance battles (persuasion, alliance-building, narrative control) are different from the skills that build valuable products (technical depth, user empathy, rapid iteration).

The result: "proposals that should take a day can often take weeks of forum posts, temperature checks, and multiple votes. Meanwhile, your competitor ships and positions themselves against the 'slow DAO.'"

This politicization failure mode extends the existing claim that [[token voting DAOs offer no minority protection beyond majority goodwill]] — even in cases where majorities aren't actively looting, the governance process itself selects against the people best equipped to create value. The problem isn't just that token voting enables extraction; it's that the governance process itself attracts and rewards the wrong type of participant.

Stani's summary is devastating: "It can often feel like we took the worst parts of corporate bureaucracy and removed the parts that create accountability in the name of decentralization." This frames current DAOs as having removed accountability mechanisms (boards, fiduciary duty, shareholder rights) while retaining bureaucratic overhead (committee processes, multi-stage voting, political dynamics) — the worst possible combination.
