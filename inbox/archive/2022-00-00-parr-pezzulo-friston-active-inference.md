# Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior

Comprehensive treatment of active inference by Parr, Pezzulo, and Friston (2022). First principles approach to understanding behavior — perception, planning, and action unified as probabilistic inference under free energy minimization.

## Key Concepts for Extraction

### Free Energy Principle (FEP)
- Any self-organizing system maintaining existence in a dynamic environment must minimize variational free energy
- Free energy = upper bound on surprise (negative log probability of sensory states given generative model)
- Two components: Accuracy (how well model explains data) and Complexity (KL-divergence penalizing deviation from priors)
- Systems minimize free energy two ways: perception (update internal model) and action (change environment to match predictions)

### Active Inference
- Unifies perception and action under single imperative
- Perception = updating beliefs to reduce prediction error
- Action = changing sensory inputs to confirm predictions
- Expected Free Energy (EFE) decomposes into epistemic value (information gain) and extrinsic value (preference satisfaction)
- Elegantly addresses exploration-exploitation tradeoff

### Markov Blankets
- Statistical boundary separating internal from external states
- Interactions only through sensory states (inflow) and active states (outflow)
- Applicable at any scale: cells, organisms, groups, institutions
- Defines what constitutes a "system" — if it has a Markov blanket, FEP applies

### Universality Claim
- FEP applies to any self-organizing system with a Markov blanket
- From chemotaxis to nervous systems to cognitive systems
- Criticized as potentially unfalsifiable (mathematical framework vs empirical theory)
- Specific instantiations remain testable even if the principle itself is meta-theoretical
