# The Free Energy Principle: A Unifying Framework Connecting Individual Existence, Collective Order, and Social Value Judgments

## Core Argument
FEP as meta-theory unifying individual existence (survival via free energy minimization), collective order (shared generative models + social norms), and social value judgments (free energy gradients as the basis of valuation). Proposes integration with LLMs for decision support systems.

## Key Claims for Extraction

### 1. Shared Generative Models Enable Collective Behavior (Section 3.2)
For stable collectives, individuals must develop partially consistent generative models and prior beliefs about shared world. When predictive models align, interactions become predictable, reducing mutual "interactive surprise." Communication = both parties adjusting to minimize surprise experienced by the other. Coordination emerges when agents can predict others' actions based on shared expectations.

### 2. Trust and Cooperation Are Low Free Energy Attractors (Section 3.2)
States of mutual trust and cooperation represent low free energy attractors for social systems — interactions more predictable, uncertainty reduced, cognitive/material costs of vigilance and conflict diminished. Social capital (networks, norms, trust) is a structure that helps collectives jointly minimize free energy.

### 3. Cultural Norms as Adaptive Generative Models (Section 3.3)
Cultural norms, traditions, customs = generative models constructed by collectives, transmitted across generations. They encode predictions about how to navigate environments and minimize surprise. Agriculture, social etiquette, moral codes = predictive strategies reducing uncertainty under specific conditions.

### 4. Institutions Stabilize Shared Generative Models (Section 3.3)
Social institutions = higher-order structures emerging during cultural evolution. They guide society toward lower free energy by stabilizing shared models, providing clear behavioral expectations (strong priors), coordinating large-scale action. BUT institutions can become rigid — when environment changes and institutions fail to update models, they increase societal free energy.

### 5. Social Value Judgments as Free Energy Gradient Assessment (Section 4.2)
Novel hypothesis: society's value judgment of a behavior/norm/state = assessment of how it affects the gradient of collective free energy. Positive value = expected to decrease collective free energy. Negative value = expected to increase it. Extends individual emotional valence (Joffily & Coricelli) to collective level.

### 6. Collective Systems Get Trapped in Local Free Energy Minima (Section 3.3)
Systems can get stuck in "path dependencies" — locally low free energy attractors that are globally suboptimal. Example: discriminatory norms that are locally stable but harm overall wellbeing. Escaping requires significant collective "surprise" (crises, social movements) or large-scale coordinated model update.

### 7. FEP's Normative vs Mechanistic Tension (Section 2.4)
Is FEP describing what systems SHOULD do (normative) or what they ACTUALLY do (mechanistic)? If normative, limited explanatory power for HOW things work. If mechanistic, needs falsifiable instantiations. This tension impacts whether FEP-based systems enforce norms or simulate processes.

### 8. LLMs as Implicit Societal Generative Models (Section 5.3)
LLMs trained on massive text encode implicit generative models of social interactions, norms, beliefs, values. Can be used to parameterize FEP models of social systems. Self-attention mechanisms could identify key drivers of social dynamics by revealing what factors the model attends to most.

## Criticisms Acknowledged
- Operationalization challenge: how to quantify "collective surprise" or construct shared generative model that reflects social dynamics
- Falsifiability: FEP at high abstraction may be unfalsifiable (mathematical framework, not empirical theory)
- Reductionism risk: emergent properties may not reduce to individual surprise minimization
- Value alignment meta-problem: who defines "preferred states" in a societal FEP model?
