# Active Inference as a Theory of Sentient Behavior

## Core Argument
Active inference provides a unifying perspective on action and perception — sentient behavior depends on organisms' implicit use of internal models to predict, infer, and act. The paper reviews the conceptual and historical roots of active inference, key developments, scope across cognitive topics, and future directions. Framed as a normative theory that formalizes the "predictive brain" idea and provides first-principle accounts of computational and neuronal processes.

## Key Concepts for Extraction

### Historical Convergence
- Three independent intellectual traditions converge on active inference:
  1. Helmholtzian inference (perception as unconscious inference)
  2. Cybernetics (goal-directed behavior via error correction)
  3. Ecological psychology (affordances, action-oriented perception)
- Active inference unifies what were previously seen as conflicting accounts

### Expected Free Energy = Pragmatic + Epistemic Value
- Expected free energy decomposes into: pragmatic imperative (utility maximization) + epistemic imperative (information gain/uncertainty resolution)
- Maps to Bayesian surprise, optimal Bayesian design, intrinsic motivation, infomax principle
- Richer than standard optimization objectives (RL reward, expected utility)

### Precision Control as Attention and Confidence
- Precision = inverse variance of beliefs/parameters
- Precision control determines relative weighting of top-down predictions vs bottom-up evidence
- Maps onto attention: high precision on prediction errors = attending to those signals
- Aberrant precision → psychopathology: too high = rigid inference/delusions, too low = overfitting to noise

### Hierarchical and Temporal Depth
- Generative models compose hierarchically (lower levels = fast dynamics, higher levels = slow context)
- Temporal depth enables planning (discrete-time models for decision sequences)
- Continuous + discrete models can be combined into hybrid architectures

### Beyond Neuroscience
- Active inference extends to robotics (self-supervised learning of world models)
- Machine learning applications (goal-directed behavior without explicit rewards)
- Psychopathology (precision failures explain delusions, anxiety, depression)
